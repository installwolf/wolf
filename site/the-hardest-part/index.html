The hardest part isn't blocking the sites. It's telling one person.

On secrecy, self-efficacy, and the order that works

It's 1 a.m. You already know which sites. You knew them a year ago. Naming them, blocking them — that was never the hard part.

The list has always been short, and you have always known every item on it. So why is it still here?

Because the problem was never that you lacked a list. It's that you've been fighting this alone, in the dark, where no one can see you win or lose. And the one move that would change that — telling a single other human being, out loud, "I have a problem I can't hold by myself" — is the exact move the entire industry is built to help you avoid.

The lock is the easy twenty percent

Walk the shelf of blockers and they are all selling the same thing: a better lock. A longer timer. A password you can't guess. A cage with fewer bars to bend. They compete on how hard it is to get out.

A good lock matters — we build one, so we'd say that. It buys you the ten seconds where the craving outruns your judgment. It turns a two-click relapse into a two-day wait. That gap is real and worth having.

But a lock is a speed bump, and every serious voice in recovery — the clinicians, the twelve-step rooms, the people three years clean — will tell you the same unglamorous thing: durable change doesn't come from the lock. It comes from the moment you stop being the only person who knows.

The lock is the easy twenty percent. It's the part a machine can do for you. The eighty percent that actually moves you is the part no software will ever do on your behalf, because it isn't technical. It's human, and it's mortifying, and it's yours.

Secrecy is the fuel

Compulsion runs on secrecy. It needs the dark. The whole architecture of it — the private tab, the cleared history, the story you tell yourself in the morning — exists to keep one fact from ever being spoken to another person: this has me, and I can't put it down.

The first step of the oldest recovery program in the world isn't "install something." It's admitted we were powerless. Admitted — to someone. Said out loud. Because the saying is not the prelude to the work; the saying is the work. The instant a real person you'd hate to disappoint knows the truth, the thing loses its favourite hiding place. You can't relapse in private anymore, because private is gone.

That's the mechanism. Not surveillance — disclosure. A partner who watches your activity helps, but the watching was never the active ingredient. The active ingredient was you deciding to be watchable. To be known. Which is exactly why it's so hard, and why so few people do it.

You can't start there

Here's the trap. If telling someone is the thing that works, the obvious advice is "go tell someone." And for most people, at 1 a.m., alone, ashamed, that advice is useless. You cannot jump from no one knows to I told my wife in a single step. The drop is too far.

What's missing in that gap isn't information. You have all the information. What's missing is the quiet, unglamorous belief that you could actually do this — that if you reached out, you'd be reaching out as someone with a fighting chance, not as someone about to fail in public and prove the worst thing you already suspect about yourself.

Psychologists have a name for that belief: self-efficacy. And they've known for decades where it comes from. Not from pep talks. Not from deciding to try harder. It's built almost entirely out of small, real, accumulated wins — evidence, gathered quietly, that you are in fact capable of the thing you're afraid you're not.

So there is a rung below telling someone, and it's this: get a little proof, for your own eyes only, that you can hold the line — before you ask anyone else to hold it with you.

The wrong way to build proof

This is where most tools reach for the streak. The day counter. The badge. The number that climbs and then, one bad night, crashes to zero.

Be careful with that number. It lies in two directions.

It lies when it's high, because it counts the wrong thing. A blocker knows it's running; it doesn't know you're free. A thirty-day streak can be thirty days of the software working perfectly while you route around it on your phone. Proof built on a proxy is just a more confident lie.

And it lies when it breaks. The moment the streak resets to zero, it hands you the exact story that feeds the whole cycle: see — you're back to nothing, you were never really doing this. That isn't evidence of capability. It's a shame engine with a countdown, and it manufactures the very feeling the confession was supposed to dissolve.

Real proof looks different. It isn't a streak; it's a record of the choices you actually made. You made the rule harder on yourself. You added the site you'd been avoiding adding. You started to tear the whole thing down one night — and then you didn't. Those aren't points. They're facts about who you are when it counts, and a relapse doesn't erase a single one of them. That's the kind of proof you can build alone, in private, with no one watching — until you're ready for someone to.

The order that works

So here is the whole shape of it, and it is almost the reverse of what the shelf sells you.

First, a lock — to buy the time. Then, quietly, proof — the small, honest wins that let you believe you're someone who can. And then, when the belief is solid enough to carry it, the sentence: I have a problem, and I need you to know. Said to one person whose face you'd have to see. That's the rung the other two were always building toward.

We make a blocker, and we'll be the first to tell you the blocker is the easy part. It holds the door for a couple of days. What you do with those days — get honest with yourself, and then get seen — is the part that's actually yours, and the only part that lasts.

We're not clinicians and this isn't treatment; if you're in real trouble, tell a professional, not only a friend. But whoever it is, the move that ends the private war is the same one you've been avoiding since 1 a.m. Not a better lock.

Telling one person.
